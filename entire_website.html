About Helping Hands Penan

A registered non-profit social enterprise run entirely by volunteers, dedicated to empowering the indigenous Penan people of Sarawak, Malaysian Borneo.

Our Journey

How We Started

Helping Hands Penan began over 8 years ago from a simple conversation among expatriate wives who wanted to help indigenous families traveling to town for medical care. What started as selling rattan crafts to provide income has grown into a comprehensive mission.

Registered officially in Malaysia on February 12, 2016, we operate purely through volunteer efforts with minimal overhead costs.

Our Approach

How We Help

We are driven by twin goals: catering to short-term basic needs while empowering the Penan to become self-sufficient in the long run.

The proceeds from selling handwoven craft fund essential items like solar lights for settlements without power, chainsaws and rice milling machines for livelihoods, transportation costs for students, school supplies, and monthly allowances for those pursuing higher education.

We work directly with Penan communities, making regular trips to remote villages to purchase their handicrafts at prices they set themselves, ensuring fair compensation and dignity.

Our Values

Empowerment

Transparency

Our Beneficiaries

Helping Hands Penan directly supports Penan communities across remote settlements in Sarawak, focusing on women weavers, students, and families in need.

Women Weavers

We work with skilled Penan women who use traditional weaving techniques to create beautiful bags and baskets. These women, many of whom did not receive formal education, earn sustainable income to support their families. We purchase their craft at fair prices they determine themselves, providing economic independence and dignity.

Students & Youth

Through our Education Sponsorship Programme, we support Penan students from primary school through tertiary education. We provide transportation from remote jungle settlements to urban schools, school supplies, uniforms, and monthly allowances for those pursuing higher education. This addresses the typically high dropout rates caused by transportation difficulties and financial constraints.

Remote Communities

We fund infrastructure improvements for settlements deep in the rainforest, including solar lighting systems for villages without power generators, clean water installations, chainsaws for sustainable forestry work, and rice milling machines to process their harvests.

Families in Need

We assist Penan families who travel to town for medical care, often staying at halfway houses while receiving treatment. We provide immediate support for basic needs while creating opportunities for long-term self-sufficiency.

Community Impact

Measurable Change

Over the years, we've witnessed vast improvements in the lives and financial situations of our beneficiaries. Women who were once economically dependent are now earning regular income. Children who would have dropped out of school are graduating and pursuing careers.

Our long-term goal is to encourage the younger generation we're sponsoring to eventually take over the marketing and management of the craft business, creating a truly sustainable cycle of empowerment.

The Penan Tribe

One of the last nomadic peoples in the world, the Penan of Sarawak possess deep wisdom about the rainforest and a gentle way of life that embodies profound respect for nature.

Penan Heritage

A People of the Forest

The Penan are indigenous to the forested region of upper Baram in northern Sarawak, Malaysian Borneo. Traditionally nomadic hunter-gatherers, they have lived in harmony with the rainforest for generations.

They are noted for their practice of 'Molong'—a philosophy of never taking more than necessary from nature, embodying sustainable living long before it became a modern concept.

Traditional Knowledge

Guardians of Ancient Wisdom

The Penan possess vast knowledge of the rainforest, especially its healing and medicinal properties. They are skilled craftspeople, creating fine bags, baskets, mats, spears, and blowpipes using traditional techniques passed down through generations.

Their simple way of life embodies elusive values like deep spiritual respect for nature and strong communal sharing, with little emphasis on individual ownership.

Modern Challenges

A People at a Crossroads

Since the 1950s, the Penan have faced tremendous challenges. Rampant logging in their ancestral forests has devastated their ability to sustain themselves through traditional hunting and gathering.

Most Penan communities have transitioned from nomadic life to settlement in longhouse-based villages, adapting to a more sedentary lifestyle while trying to preserve their cultural identity and skills.

Living far from towns, they face economic marginalization and limited access to education and healthcare. The loss of forest resources has made their traditional way of life increasingly difficult to maintain.

Supporting Cultural Continuity

Helping Hands Penan works to support the Penan in their transition while preserving their valuable traditional skills and knowledge. By creating market opportunities for their exceptional craftwork, we help ensure that Penan weaving traditions continue to thrive and provide economic benefit to future generations.
